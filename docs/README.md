# OctoAcme Project Management Docs

Welcome to the centralized documentation for OctoAcme's project management processes. This README serves as the entry point for onboarding new team members and navigating our process documentation. The docs here capture the workflows, roles, and practices that enable consistent, repeatable project delivery across OctoAcme's engineering and product teams. Keeping this knowledge centralized reduces ramp-up time, closes process gaps, and supports alignment across all cross-functional initiatives.

## OctoAcme Project Management Processes

OctoAcme follows a structured project lifecycle that begins with **initiation** and flows through **planning**, **execution & tracking**, and culminates in **release & deployment** and **retrospectives**. During initiation, a Project One-pager is created to define the problem statement, SMART objectives, success metrics, stakeholders, and an initial risk list. A formal decision gate confirms stakeholder alignment and team availability before moving into planning. In the planning phase, the team holds a kickoff meeting, builds a prioritized backlog with acceptance criteria, defines the Definition of Done, maps out release milestones, and logs cross-team dependencies in the Risk Register. This front-loaded rigor ensures the delivery team has a clear, shared understanding before a single line of code is written.

Execution is managed through an iterative, increment-based rhythm anchored by daily standups, weekly delivery syncs, and end-of-sprint demos. Work moves through a structured project board (Backlog → Ready → In Progress → In Review → QA → Done), and pull requests are kept small, linked to issues, and gated on passing CI checks and at least one peer review. Quality is embedded throughout: unit tests cover new logic, integration tests validate component interactions, end-to-end smoke tests guard critical flows, and security scanning runs in CI. Blockers are escalated through three defined levels—team standup, PM/Product Lead escalation, and sponsor-level intervention—so issues are surfaced and resolved quickly.

Risks and stakeholder communication are managed proactively. A Risk Register tracks each risk by ID, description, impact, likelihood, owner, mitigation plan, and status, and is reviewed at every weekly sync. Stakeholder groups receive regular updates (weekly or milestone-based) via a single source of truth, using a standard Weekly Status Template that covers progress, next steps, risks & blockers, and decisions needed. Escalation paths are explicit—from team level up through PM, Product Lead, and Sponsor—and security incidents follow a dedicated runbook. Release and deployment are standardized across patch, minor, and major release types, each requiring passing CI and security scans, drafted release notes, a rollback plan, and staged smoke tests before production deployment.

After each sprint, release, or major milestone, the team holds a structured retrospective covering what went well, what could be improved, and concrete action items with owners and due dates. Action items are tracked in the project backlog and reviewed in the weekly PM sync to ensure follow-through. This continuous improvement loop is reinforced by a culture that measures the impact of changes, celebrates incremental progress, and uses data-informed decisions to iterate on both product and process. Roles are clearly defined: the **Project Manager** coordinates delivery, schedules, risk, and communications; the **Product Manager** owns the product vision, backlog prioritization, and success metrics; and **Developers** implement features, write tests, and contribute to design and code review—ensuring clear ownership and accountability at every stage.
